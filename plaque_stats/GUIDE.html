Plaque Stats & Violins guide

Turn grouped plaque measurements into publication-ready violin SuperPlots + statistics. One engine, three front-ends (browser app, command line, R). This guide walks through a real worked example.

Companion to plaque_stats/README.md (full reference). The example below was produced by running the tool on a small example dataset — four samples, three plates each.

1 · Put your data in this shape

One row per plaque. A tidy CSV (or TSV/Excel) with a group column (the sample/ condition you compare), a replicate column (the plate — the experimental unit), and one or more measurement columns. Both apps auto-detect the columns.

  • group — the sample / condition / phage (the x-axis categories).
  • replicate — the plate a plaque came from. With it, statistics run per plate (avoiding pseudoreplication); without it you get a warning.
  • measurements — any numeric columns (diameter_mm, area_mm2, turbidity…); you pick which to plot.

2 · Run it (any of three ways)

Browser app
Double-click Run Analysis App.bat (or run plaque-stats-app). Upload your file, pick the columns, tweak options, download the figure + tables.
Command line
Scriptable / reproducible — the exact command used for this example is below.
R-Shiny
Double-click Run R App.bat — same options, same numbers.
python plaque_stats.py data.csv \
    --group group --value diameter_mm --replicate replicate \
    --out results --title "Plaque diameter by sample" --ylabel "Plaque diameter (mm)"

3 · What you get — the worked example

How to read it — what to look for
  • Do the plate means agree? Tightly clustered plate markers = reproducible across plates; scattered plate markers = high plate-to-plate variability (usually your real source of noise).
  • Is a whole plate an outlier? One plate mean sitting far from the others is worth checking (a bad pour, focus, or contamination) — it drives the group's error bar.
  • Is the group difference bigger than the plate scatter? If the black group markers differ by more than the plate markers spread within a group, the difference is likely real.
  • The violin's shape — symmetric vs skewed; one hump or two (a bimodal violin can hint at two plaque phenotypes).
  • n (top of each group) is plaques measured — but the statistics use the plate means, not the individual plaques. Here's why. ↓

4 · The statistics — and why it’s the plate, not the plaque

The plate is the experimental unit, so by default every test runs on per-plate means, not on individual plaques — pooling plaques is pseudoreplication and inflates significance. The test is chosen for you:

Comparison~NormalNon-normal
2 groupsWelch’s t-test (+ Cohen’s d)Mann–Whitney U
>2 groupsone-way ANOVA + Tukey HSD (+ η²)Kruskal–Wallis + Dunn (Holm)

At small replicate n (< 8/group — the usual case) the tool defaults to the parametric test on the plate means, following the SuperPlots convention (Shapiro–Wilk is unreliable and non-parametric tests are underpowered at n = 3). In this example: one-way ANOVA, p = 0.001, η² = 0.84.

What to be aware of (the common pitfalls)
  • Pseudoreplication — the #1 mistake. Plaques on one plate are not independent; treating them as your sample size hugely inflates significance. Aggregate to plate means (this tool does, by default). See the demo below.
  • Significance ≠ importance — with enough plates a tiny difference becomes "significant." Report the effect size (η² / Cohen's d, in report.md) and the actual magnitude, not just p.
  • Small n — with <8 plates/group the tool uses a parametric test on the plate means (Shapiro is unreliable and non-parametric is underpowered at n=3). Aim for ≥3 plates; more = more power.
  • Uneven plates — a group with one 300-plaque plate and two 20-plaque plates is dominated by the big plate at the plaque level; the plate-mean approach weights each plate equally (correct).
  • Size definition — "diameter" is the area-equivalent diameter d = 2√(A/π), halo-inclusive (an upper bound on the sharp clear zone). State it in Methods.
