01Pairs and differences — the raw material

Everything starts from one idea: for each plaque you have two numbers that should be the same — the Toolkit's diameter and Fiji's diameter — and the single most useful thing you can do is subtract them.

The difference for a plaque is tool − fiji. A difference of 0 means perfect agreement. A positive difference means the Toolkit read bigger; a negative one means Fiji read bigger. Three real plaques from your set:

# diameter d = 2·√(area/π); difference = tool − fiji plaque #1 : tool 1.9149 fiji 1.9136 → diff = +0.0013 mm (they basically agreed) plaque #11 : tool 2.0342 fiji 2.1982 → diff = −0.1640 mm (Toolkit smaller — a red outlier) plaque #92 : tool 0.6482 fiji 0.8361 → diff = −0.1879 mm (smallest plaque, hardest to trace)

You now have 100 differences. Every statistic on this page is just a different summary of those 100 numbers. If you understand the differences, you understand all of it.

02Bias — the average difference

Bias is simply the average of your 100 differences. Add them all up, divide by 100:

bias = (diff₁ + diff₂ + … + diff₁₀₀) / 100 = −0.0285 mm = −1.8% of the average plaque diameter (≈1.56 mm)

What it means: across all your plaques, the Toolkit reads on average 0.0285 mm smaller than your hand tracing — about 1.8% low. Bias is about direction and average size of the disagreement. A bias near zero means neither method is systematically bigger.

Real vs. important — an honest nuance Is that −1.8% just luck of the draw? A paired t‑test says no: p = 6.6×10⁻⁶ — the offset is statistically real and consistent. But "statistically real" is not the same as "big enough to care about." With 100 plaques you can detect a 0.03 mm shift that is far smaller than you could reliably re‑place a caliper by hand. So the honest reading is: there is a small, genuine tendency to read ~1.8% low — tiny for most plaque biology, and if it ever matters you can simply subtract it.

03Spread — the SD of the differences

Bias is the average difference, but individual plaques scatter around that average. Plaque #1 was +0.001; plaque #92 was −0.188. The standard deviation (SD) of the differences measures that scatter — the typical wobble around the bias.

# for each plaque: (its diff − bias)²; sum them; divide by n−1 = 99; take the √ # (n−1, not n — this is the "sample" SD, which is what limits of agreement use) SD of the 100 differences = 0.0598 mm

On its own the SD is just an ingredient — but it's the ingredient that turns the average bias into a statement about any single plaque, which is what you actually care about. That's the next section.

0495% Limits of Agreement — the headline number

This is the number to quote in a paper, because it answers the practical question: “if I measure one plaque with the Toolkit instead of by hand, how far off could I be?”

Take the bias and step out 1.96 standard deviations either side of it:

lower limit = bias − 1.96 × SD = −0.0285 − 1.96 × 0.0598 = −0.146 mm upper limit = bias + 1.96 × SD = −0.0285 + 1.96 × 0.0598 = +0.089 mm
Say it in words For any single plaque, the Toolkit's diameter lands within −0.15 to +0.09 mm of the hand measurement 95% of the time. On a typical ~1.5 mm plaque that band is about −10% to +6%.

Where does 1.96 come from? When the differences are approximately bell‑shaped, 95% of them fall within 1.96 SD of the mean — the standard "95%" multiplier from the normal distribution (2 is the round‑number version). The limits are a normal‑based approximation (here 92 of your 100 differences land inside them, close to the expected 95%); the bias itself needs no such assumption. That's the whole idea behind Bland & Altman's method: bias tells you the systematic shift, the limits tell you the random scatter, and together they describe a single future measurement.

05Reading the Bland–Altman plot

The Bland–Altman plot is just those differences, drawn. For each plaque:

  • x‑axis = the average of the two methods (tool + fiji)/2 — your best guess of the plaque's true size.
  • y‑axis = the difference tool − fiji — how much they disagreed.

Why plot difference‑vs‑mean instead of tool‑vs‑fiji? Because a tool‑vs‑fiji scatter squashes everything onto the identity line and hides the disagreement. Plotting the difference blows the disagreement up to full height so you can see (a) is it centred on zero, and (b) does it fan out with size.

06Proportional bias — does the gap grow with plaque size?

A single "limits of agreement" band is only fair if the disagreement is the same size for small and large plaques. If big plaques disagreed more, the band would be too wide for small ones and too narrow for big ones. So we test it: fit a straight line through the Bland–Altman cloud (difference vs mean) and look at its slope.

slope of (difference vs mean) = +0.006 mm per mm p = 0.78 → not different from flat Spearman ρ (difference vs size) = +0.03 p = 0.79

The slope is essentially zero and nowhere near significant — no proportional bias. The Toolkit's small −1.8% offset is the same whether a plaque is 0.6 mm or 3 mm, so one set of limits is valid across your whole range.

A note on "slope 0.98 vs 1.006" You may see a second slope quoted: the ordinary regression of Toolkit on Fiji gives 0.98 (statistically indistinguishable from a perfect 1.0; p = 0.46). An earlier draft quoted 1.006 — that was a different regression (Fiji‑area on Toolkit‑area). Both say the same thing (no size dependence); the app and figure use the diameter value 0.98. They differ only because ordinary regression depends on which variable you call "x" and whether you use area or diameter — which is exactly why the difference‑vs‑mean test above is the cleaner, direction‑free check.

07ICC — one number for "do they agree?"

The Intraclass Correlation Coefficient rolls agreement into a single 0–1 score. Yours is 0.974. Here's what it's really measuring and why it's stricter (and more honest) than a plain correlation.

ICC asks: of all the variation in your measurements, how much is real plaque‑to‑plaque difference versus measurement disagreement? If plaques genuinely differ a lot in size and the two methods barely disagree, ICC is near 1. Written as variance:

(between‑plaque variance) − (error) ICC = ─────────────────────────────────────────────── (between‑plaque) + (error) + (method‑bias)

The pieces come from a two‑way ANOVA of your data (plaques × method). Your actual numbers:

Mean squares from the two‑way ANOVA (targets = 100 plaques, raters = 2 methods). ICC(A,1) = absolute agreement, single measure — the correct variant when you want the two methods to give the same value, not just track each other.

08Pearson r and its famous trap

Pearson's r (yours = 0.979) measures whether the two methods move together — when Fiji says a plaque is bigger, does the Toolkit too? It's a fine correlation number, but on its own it can badly mislead about agreement:

The trap

Imagine the Toolkit read exactly double every plaque. Pearson r would be a perfect 1.00 — they move together flawlessly — yet the tool is 100% too big and totally useless. r cannot see a constant offset or scaling.

The fix

Bland–Altman (bias + limits) and ICC do penalise offsets and scaling. In the ×2 case ICC would drop dramatically to a poor value even though Pearson r stays a perfect 1.00. That's exactly why a proper validation reports r and Bland–Altman and ICC — not r alone.

So r = 0.979 tells you the two methods track each other tightly; the ICC and limits then confirm they also land on the same value. All three pointing the same way is what makes the result solid.

09RMSE & MAE — the typical miss, in millimetres

These two put the disagreement back into plain millimetres:

  • MAE (mean absolute error) = 0.049 mm — the average size of a disagreement, ignoring direction. "A typical plaque is off by about 0.05 mm."
  • RMSE (root‑mean‑square error) = 0.066 mm — similar, but squares the differences first so it punishes the occasional big miss more. RMSE ≥ MAE always (equal only if every plaque missed by the same amount); the gap between them tells you a few plaques (your red ones) miss by more than the rest.

On ~1.5 mm plaques, a 0.05 mm typical miss is about 3% — the same story the limits told, in a single headline number.

10Why 2 plates is the soft spot (pseudoreplication)

Everything above uses n = 100 plaques, which is a strong sample — it makes all these numbers tight and trustworthy for the size question. The caveat is not the plaque count; it's that those 100 plaques sit on only 2 plates.

Plaques on the same plate share one calibration, one focus, one lighting, one operator session — so they aren't 100 fully independent draws. Statisticians call leaning on that pseudoreplication. Two consequences:

  • For size agreement (this page): it matters less, because both methods measure the same pixels on the same image — you're comparing two rulers on identical objects, so a shared plate can't fake agreement.
  • For generalisation and for plaque counts: it matters more — 2 plates can't show the result holds across the range of conditions you'll actually meet.
Recommendation Report it honestly as “n = 100 plaques from 2 plates” — that's a legitimate local size validation. To make it bulletproof for a reviewer, spread a similar total across ≥ 5 plates covering your real range (sparse→crowded, small→large), and ideally have a second person trace some in Fiji. The plate is the experimental unit; plaque counts need plate‑level replicates regardless.

11Report it · reproduce it yourself

The one‑sentence result for your paper

Plaque diameters from Plaque Toolkit agreed with independent manual Fiji/ImageJ tracing (n = 100 plaques from 2 plates): Pearson r = 0.98, ICC = 0.97; Bland–Altman mean bias −0.03 mm (≈1.8%), 95% limits of agreement −0.15 to +0.09 mm, with no proportional bias (difference‑vs‑mean slope +0.006, p = 0.78).

Do the whole thing by hand in four steps

You never have to take these on trust. With your two columns of numbers:

  1. Differences: subtract, plaque by plaque (tool − fiji).
  2. Bias: average the differences → −0.028.
  3. SD: standard deviation of the differences → 0.060.
  4. Limits: bias ± 1.96 × SD → −0.146 and +0.089. Done.

ICC and r need a couple more lines of arithmetic (shown in §7 and available in any spreadsheet or the app), but bias and the limits — the numbers you'll actually quote — are four steps you can check on paper.

Or let the app do it

In Plaque Toolkit → Fiji agreement tab: paste your Toolkit column and your Fiji column, pick diameter or area, and press Compute. It prints the bias, limits, ICC, r and slope, draws the exact plot above (with the red outliers), and writes the paste‑ready sentence — the same maths as this page.
